Planalto Mirandês

In these photos, I tell the story of Planalto Mirandes, a region in north-eastern Portugal that offered me hospitality for nine months.


In Planalto, the eye lazes serenely over shrubs and is free to wander through boundless spaces in a land of bright, dazzling colours, with the scent of hay and amber, where time has taken a pause and space seeks rest. There are no obstacles on the horizon, you are miles away from the cities: from those dreadful concrete flows, from the war theatres of lights and horns, from vacuous, undefined and transient lives. Silence accompanies the days and transports you to places populated by a sacred stillness, where the waters flow abundantly and freely and the concert of nature plays its part. Lichens, small but important environmental indicators, adorn and cling to the trees like sentinels of the air.
Shepherds with their flocks and donkey breeders walk and enliven the narrow streets of Atenor, Vilar Seco, and Aguas Vivas, small villages on the plateau. However, this region is mainly populated by elderly people whose lives and work - mostly handicrafts and land-related trades - are being lost. The Miranda plateau is said to represent the decadence of the country's countryside: farming is a very difficult and unprofitable activity and young people flee to the city, contributing to the depopulation of the villages. To safeguard these legacies, the AEPGA (Associação para o Estudo e Protecção do Gado Asinino) association is mainly concerned with the preservation of the Miranda donkey, its promotion and its valorisation, not only as a genetic heritage but also as an important cultural, relational and social asset. In fact, today a double extinction is taking place: once donkey breeders die, donkeys also risk extinction. With its work, AEPGA connects different sections of the Mirandan landscape and fragments of a time that is slipping away, repopulating the collective and social imaginary, leveraging the present to revive a past rich in memories and traditions. Traditions that are also born from details, from particulars: the stone steps and fountains, the ancient handles, the wicker baskets and the empty alley but at the same time so full. Full and laden with smells and customs, small and personal in their conformation unconcerned with the outside world. What is needed today are farmers and craftsmen, people who can sew and carve, who talk to the trees and recognise the flowers. What year of growth? We must slow down and stop, asking ourselves what is happening around us. Let’s give meaning to the wind that blows, to a poem, to silence, to the grass and the insect that passes us by. Let us approach the barn and listen to the braying of a donkey, the barking of a dog or the bleating of a sheep where in the placidity of the animals' gaze the wisdom of nature still speaks.
